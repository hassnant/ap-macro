Unit 1 — Basic Economic Concepts

Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

U1▼

Plain English

The PPC shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce given all available resources and technology. It's bowed outward (concave to origin) because of the law of increasing opportunity costs — resources aren't perfectly adaptable between uses.

Why It Matters

FRQs ask you to draw a PPC, identify efficient/inefficient/unattainable points, calculate opportunity costs, and show economic growth (outward shift) or decline (inward shift).

Common Mistakes

Drawing a straight line instead of a bowed-out curve (straight = constant opportunity cost, wrong!)
Confusing a point inside the curve (inefficient, achievable) with a point outside (unattainable)
Saying a point inside the curve means "resources are scarce" — every point on the curve also faces scarcity
🧠 BOWED OUT = increasing opportunity cost. Growth → curve shifts OUTWARD. Disaster → INWARD.

Comparative Advantage & Trade

U1▼

Plain English

Comparative advantage = producing where your opportunity cost is lower. Absolute advantage = producing more with the same resources. Trade allows both parties to consume beyond their individual PPCs.

How to Find Comparative Advantage

  • Calculate opportunity cost for each good in each country
  • The country with the LOWER opportunity cost has the comparative advantage
  • Terms of trade must be between the two opportunity costs for both to benefit

Common Mistakes

Picking the country that produces MORE (absolute advantage) instead of lower opportunity cost (comparative advantage)
Forgetting that terms of trade must be BETWEEN the two countries' opportunity costs
🧠 Give up LESS of X to get Y → comparative advantage in Y. It's about what you sacrifice, not what you produce.

Circular Flow & Injections vs. Leakages

U1▼

Plain English

The circular flow shows how money moves between households, firms, and markets. Households supply factors (labor, capital) to firms and buy goods from firms. Money flows in a circle through product and factor markets.

Injections vs. Leakages

  • Injections add money to the flow: Investment (I), Government Spending (G), Exports (X)
  • Leakages remove money: Saving (S), Taxes (T), Imports (M)
  • Equilibrium: I + G + X = S + T + M

Common Mistakes

Forgetting that exports are an injection (foreign spending on our goods) and imports are a leakage
Thinking the circular flow is only about goods — money flows OPPOSITE to real goods/services
🧠 Injections = IGX. Leakages = STM. At equilibrium: IGX = STM.

Unit 2 — Measurement of Economic Performance

Okun's Law & Output Gaps

U2▼

Okun's Law

For every 1% that unemployment rises above the Natural Rate of Unemployment, real GDP falls approximately 2% below potential. This quantifies the cost of cyclical unemployment in terms of lost output.

How to Use It

  • If NRU = 4% and actual unemployment = 6%, cyclical unemployment = 2%
  • Output gap ≈ 2 × 2% = 4% below potential GDP
  • If potential GDP = $2,000B, actual GDP ≈ $1,920B

Common Mistakes

Applying Okun's Law to all unemployment types — it only applies to CYCLICAL unemployment
Confusing the output gap direction — higher unemployment → lower output (they move inversely)
🧠 Okun: 1% more cyclical u = 2% less real GDP. "One point up, two points down."

GDP & National Accounts

U2▼

Plain English

GDP = market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a year. Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + NX

The Four Components

  • C (Consumption) — household spending; largest (~70% of US GDP)
  • I (Investment) — business spending on capital goods; NOT stocks or bonds
  • G (Government) — government purchases ONLY; NOT transfer payments
  • NX (Net Exports) — Exports − Imports; can be negative

Real vs. Nominal GDP

Nominal uses current prices (inflated). Real adjusts for inflation using a base year. Always compare with Real GDP across time periods.

Common Mistakes

Including transfer payments (SS, welfare) in G — they are NOT government purchases of goods/services
Double-counting intermediate goods — only count FINAL goods
Confusing GNP (by a country's nationals anywhere) with GDP (within borders)
🧠 "Can I Get aNeXt raise?" → C + I + G + NX

Unemployment Types & Natural Rate

U2▼

The Four Types

  • Frictional — between jobs, short-term, healthy (job search, new grads)
  • Structural — skills mismatch from technology/industry change; requires retraining
  • Cyclical — from recessions; demand for workers falls
  • Seasonal — predictable patterns (lifeguards, tax accountants)

Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU)

NRU = Frictional + Structural. At full employment, cyclical = 0. Typically ~4–5%. The economy is AT the LRAS when unemployment equals the NRU.

Common Mistakes

Thinking full employment = 0% unemployment — it means cyclical = 0, not total = 0
Saying discouraged workers are "unemployed" — they left the labor force and are NOT counted
🧠 NRU = Frictional + Structural (always there). Cyclical = the problem part. Full employment means cyclical = 0.

CPI, GDP Deflator & Inflation

U2▼

CPI vs. GDP Deflator

  • CPI — fixed basket of consumer goods; used for cost of living
  • GDP Deflator — all domestically produced goods; broader; used to convert nominal → real GDP

Key Formulas

CPI = (Current cost of basket / Base year cost) × 100
Inflation Rate = (CPI₂ − CPI₁) / CPI₁ × 100%
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

Common Mistakes

Using CPI to deflate GDP — use the GDP Deflator for that
Forgetting CPI uses a FIXED basket (doesn't account for substitution bias)
🧠 Deflate the Nominal to get the Real: Real GDP = Nominal ÷ Deflator × 100

Unit 3 — National Income & Price Determination

AD/AS Model — The Central Model

U3▼

The Three Curves

  • AD — Downward sloping. Wealth effect, interest rate effect, foreign purchases effect
  • SRAS — Upward sloping. Firms supply more as prices rise (covers costs)
  • LRAS — Vertical at potential (full-employment) GDP. Long-run price level doesn't affect output

AD Shifters (↑ → right shift)

  • ↑ Consumer/business confidence or spending
  • ↑ Government spending, ↓ taxes
  • Currency depreciation → ↑NX
  • Lower interest rates → ↑I

SRAS Shifters (↑ costs → left shift)

  • ↑ Wages, ↑ oil/resource prices, ↑ business taxes → SRAS left
  • ↑ Productivity, ↓ input costs → SRAS right

Common Mistakes

Shifting LRAS in response to a demand shock — only technology/resources shift LRAS
Drawing SRAS as vertical (that's LRAS!) or drawing LRAS as sloped
🧠 LRAS = vertical = long-run potential. Short-run equilibrium = AD ∩ SRAS. Full employment = AD ∩ SRAS ∩ LRAS.

Multipliers — Spending & Tax

U3▼

MPC & MPS

MPC = ΔConsumption / ΔIncome. MPS = 1 − MPC. If MPC = 0.8: for every $1 more income, spend $0.80, save $0.20.

Spending Multiplier = 1 / MPS

MPC = 0.8 → multiplier = 5. $100B gov spending → $500B shift in AD.

Tax Multiplier = −MPC / MPS

Always negative; always 1 smaller in magnitude than spending multiplier. MPC = 0.8 → tax multiplier = −4.

Common Mistakes

Using the spending multiplier for a tax change — they're different!
Forgetting the tax multiplier is negative for a tax increase
🧠 Spending mult = 1/MPS. Tax mult = −MPC/MPS. Tax mult is always 1 less in magnitude.

Unit 4 — Financial Sector

Money Supply, Money Market & Fed Tools

U4▼

M1 vs M2

  • M1 — currency + checking deposits + traveler's checks (most liquid)
  • M2 — M1 + savings accounts + money market accounts + small CDs

Money Multiplier = 1 / Reserve Requirement

RR = 10% → MM = 10. A $1,000 deposit can create up to $10,000 in money supply through lending.

Fed Tools

  • Open Market Operations — buy bonds → inject money (most common tool); sell bonds → remove money
  • Discount Rate — rate Fed charges banks; lower → banks borrow more → Ms up
  • Reserve Requirement — rarely changed; lower RR → more money created

Common Mistakes

Confusing discount rate (Fed to banks) with federal funds rate (bank to bank, overnight)
Forgetting that buying bonds is expansionary (injects money)
🧠 BUY bonds → Ms UP → nominal rate DOWN → investment UP → AD RIGHT. "Buy = Boost economy."
📊 Required Graph: Money Market

Unit 5 — Stabilization Policy

Fiscal Policy & Crowding Out

U5▼

Fiscal Tools

  • Expansionary — ↑G or ↓T → AD shifts right → fights recession
  • Contractionary — ↓G or ↑T → AD shifts left → fights inflation

Crowding Out Effect

Gov deficit → borrows in loanable funds market → DLF shifts right → real interest rate rises → private investment falls. This partially offsets the fiscal stimulus.

Automatic Stabilizers

Unemployment insurance and progressive taxes automatically stabilize the economy without new legislation. They reduce the size of the multiplier effect.

Common Mistakes

Forgetting to show crowding out in the loanable funds market when fiscal policy is used
Confusing crowding out (loanable funds, real rate) with money market (nominal rate)
🧠 Crowding out chain: Gov borrows → DLF right → real rate UP → private investment DOWN.

Unit 6 — International Trade & Finance

Exchange Rates & Balance of Payments

U6▼

Exchange Rates

Appreciation = currency buys MORE foreign currency. Depreciation = buys LESS.

Effects of Appreciation

  • Exports more expensive for foreigners → ↓ exports
  • Imports cheaper for citizens → ↑ imports
  • Net result: NX falls → AD shifts LEFT

Balance of Payments

  • Current Account — trade in goods/services + income + transfers
  • Capital/Financial Account — flows of financial assets (FDI, stocks, bonds)
  • Key Rule — Current Account + Capital Account = 0 (always balance)

Common Mistakes

Thinking a strong dollar is always good — it hurts exporters and net exports
Mixing up current account (trade) with capital account (financial asset flows)
🧠 Strong dollar = expensive exports = NX falls = AD LEFT. Weak dollar = NX rises = AD RIGHT.

Loanable Funds Market

U6▼

Plain English

Shows how the REAL interest rate is determined by supply (saving) and demand (borrowing for investment + government) for funds.

Critical Distinction

  • Loanable Funds → REAL interest rate
  • Money Market → NOMINAL interest rate
  • Fiscal crowding out shown in loanable funds
  • Monetary policy shown in money market
🧠 NEVER mix them: Loanable Funds = Real rate. Money Market = Nominal rate.

1. Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

Bowed-out shape showing max output combinations; growth shifts curve outward

What It Shows

Maximum production possibilities given current resources and technology. Bowed-out shape reflects increasing opportunity costs as resources are reallocated.

How to Shift

  • Outward (both axes): technology, more resources, economic growth
  • One axis only: improvement in one sector only
  • Inward: natural disaster, war, disease

2. AD/AS Model — Full Equilibrium

The most tested graph. Shows price level and real GDP at short-run and long-run equilibrium.

The Three Curves

  • AD — downward sloping; wealth, interest rate, and foreign purchases effects
  • SRAS — upward sloping; firms supply more as price level rises
  • LRAS — vertical at Yf; long-run output determined by resources, not prices

SRAS Shifters

  • ↑ Input prices → left
  • ↑ Wages → left
  • ↑ Productivity → right
  • ↓ Business taxes → right
  • Supply shocks → left (neg.) or right (pos.)

3a. AD/AS — Recessionary Gap

Y₁ < Yf · Unemployment above NRU · Expansionary policy or self-correction fixes it

What It Shows

Y₁ is LEFT of Yf — recessionary gap. Unemployment exceeds the NRU. Economy is underperforming. AD is too weak to reach potential output.

Two Fixes

  • Expansionary Policy (AD right): ↑G, ↓T, or Fed buys bonds → AD₂ → higher PL, output reaches Yf
  • Self-correction (SRAS right): Wages fall over time → SRAS shifts right → output rises, price level falls further
📋 FRQ Grader Checklist✓ E₁ clearly LEFT of LRAS ✓ Gap labeled between Y₁ and Yf ✓ AD₂ shifted right (or SRAS right for self-correction) ✓ New equilibrium E₂ at or near Yf ✓ Both equilibria labeled ✓ Price level change correctly shown

3b. AD/AS — Inflationary Gap

Y₁ > Yf · Economy "overheated" · Contractionary policy or self-correction fixes it

What It Shows

Y₁ is RIGHT of Yf — inflationary gap. Unemployment is below NRU. Wages and prices are rising. The economy is "overheated" and unsustainable.

Two Fixes

  • Contractionary Policy (AD left): ↓G, ↑T, Fed sells bonds → AD₂ → PL falls, output returns to Yf
  • Self-correction (SRAS left): Wages rise → SRAS shifts left → price level rises further, output falls to Yf
📋 FRQ Grader Checklist✓ E₁ RIGHT of LRAS ✓ Gap labeled ✓ AD shifts left OR SRAS shifts left ✓ New E₂ at Yf ✓ Price level change shown correctly ✓ Both equilibria labeled

3c. AD/AS — Long-Run Self-Correction

No policy needed · Wages adjust · Economy returns to Yf on its own over time

How It Works

In a recessionary gap, high unemployment puts downward pressure on wages. As wages fall, production costs drop → SRAS shifts right → output rises toward Yf without any government intervention.

Key Exam Points

  • Self-correction is SLOW — wages are sticky downward
  • Keynesian view: don't wait — use policy. Classical view: let markets correct.
  • Result: same real output (Yf), but lower price level than the AD-fix path
  • Inflationary gap self-corrects the same way but SRAS shifts LEFT (wages rise)
📋 FRQ Grader Checklist✓ E₁ off Yf ✓ SRAS₂ shifts in correct direction (right for recession, left for inflation) ✓ New E₂ exactly at LRAS ✓ Price level change correctly shown ✓ No AD shift drawn ✓ Both SRAS curves labeled

4. Money Market

Determines nominal interest rate; Ms is vertical; Fed policy shifts Ms

Key Points

  • Ms is VERTICAL — Fed sets money supply, not sensitive to interest rate
  • Md slopes down — lower rates = lower opportunity cost of holding cash
  • Expansionary: Fed buys bonds → Ms right → nominal rate FALLS
  • Contractionary: Fed sells bonds → Ms left → nominal rate RISES

Md Shifters (→ right)

  • ↑ Price level (need more money for same purchases)
  • ↑ Real GDP (more transactions)
  • ↓ Use of credit cards/electronic payment

5. Loanable Funds Market

Determines real interest rate; fiscal crowding out shown here (NOT the money market)

Key Points

  • REAL interest rate on Y-axis (not nominal!)
  • SLF upward — higher real rate → more saving incentive
  • DLF downward — higher rate → less investment demand
  • Crowding out: Gov deficit → DLF right → r rises → private investment falls

What Shifts SLF Right?

  • ↑ Household savings (higher income, lower time preference)
  • Foreign capital inflows (↑ foreign interest in US assets)
  • ↑ Government budget surplus

6. Phillips Curve (SR & LR)

Inflation-unemployment trade-off; stagflation shifts SRPC right; LRPC vertical at NRU

Key Points

  • SRPC: inverse relationship — lower unemployment → higher inflation
  • LRPC: vertical at NRU — no long-run trade-off
  • Move along SRPC: AD policy (expansionary → left, toward A)
  • Shift SRPC: supply shocks, changed expectations

Stagflation Effect

  • Negative supply shock → SRAS left → SRPC shifts RIGHT
  • Both inflation AND unemployment rise simultaneously
  • Policy dilemma: can't fix both with AD policy alone

7. Circular Flow Diagram

Shows how households, firms, and markets interact; injections and leakages

What It Shows

  • Two key players: Households (consumers/workers) and Firms (producers/employers)
  • Two key markets: Product Market (goods/services) and Factor Market (labor, capital, land)
  • Real flows (goods, labor) move opposite to money flows (spending, wages)

Injections vs. Leakages

  • Injections add to the flow: Investment (I), Government Spending (G), Exports (X)
  • Leakages remove from the flow: Saving (S), Taxes (T), Imports (M)
  • Equilibrium: Total injections = Total leakages

8. Balance of Payments — Current & Capital Account

Conceptual diagram showing how the two accounts always sum to zero

The Golden Rule

  • Current Account + Capital Account = 0 (always, by accounting identity)
  • A current account deficit MUST be financed by a capital account surplus
  • US trade deficit → foreigners hold dollars → invest in US stocks, bonds, real estate

Common Scenarios

  • US runs trade deficit (imports > exports) → current account deficit
  • Offset: foreigners invest in US assets → capital account surplus
  • Higher US interest rates → more foreign investment → capital account improves → current account can worsen
📋 FRQ Grader Checklist✓ Both accounts labeled (Current + Capital/Financial) ✓ Relationship: they sum to zero ✓ Deficit in one = surplus in the other ✓ Example: trade deficit → foreigners invest in US = capital surplus ✓ Link to exchange rates correctly explained

9. Reserve Market — Ample vs. Limited Reserves

The Fed's modern framework; IOR sets a floor on the federal funds rate in the ample-reserves regime

Two Regimes

  • Limited Reserves (left of dashed line): Traditional framework. Fed funds rate determined by supply & demand. Reserve scarcity gives the Fed leverage to move rates.
  • Ample Reserves (right of dashed line): Post-2008 framework. Banks hold excess reserves. The IOR (Interest on Reserves) acts as a hard floor — no bank will lend below what the Fed pays on reserves.

How the Fed Controls Rates Now

  • IOR (Interest on Reserves): Fed pays banks to hold reserves. Raising IOR → FFR rises. Lowering IOR → FFR falls.
  • SR₀ stays fixed: With ample reserves, shifting supply has little effect — IOR is the binding tool.
  • Target range: Fed sets a corridor (IOR floor, Discount Rate ceiling) and keeps FFR inside it.
  • DR₁ and IOR₁ show the entire corridor shifting up — a rate hike.

10. Foreign Exchange Market

Supply and demand for a currency; appreciation (D right) and depreciation (S right)

Key Points

  • Exchange rate on Y-axis (price of domestic currency)
  • D$ slopes down: lower price → foreigners buy more USD
  • S$ slopes up: higher price → more USD sellers
  • D$ right → appreciation; S$ right → depreciation

What Shifts D$?

  • ↑ US interest rates → foreigners buy USD to invest → D$ right → appreciation
  • ↑ US inflation → foreign demand for US goods falls → D$ left → depreciation
  • ↑ Foreign income → more US exports → D$ right

Formulas & Numbers

⚠️ There is NO formula sheet on the AP Macro exam. Memorize all of these.

GDP Formulas

Expenditure Approach to GDP

GDP = C + I + G + NX

C=Consumption, I=Investment, G=Gov purchases (NOT transfers), NX=Exports−Imports

⚠ Transfer payments (welfare, SS) are NOT in G

Real GDP

Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100

Use real GDP to compare across time periods. Eliminates inflation distortion.

GDP Deflator

GDP Deflator = (Nominal / Real) × 100

Broader than CPI — covers all domestically produced goods. Used to convert nominal to real.

Economic Growth Rate

Growth = (GDP₂ − GDP₁) / GDP₁ × 100%

Always use Real GDP for meaningful comparisons over time.

Inflation & Price Level

Consumer Price Index (CPI)

CPI = (Cost_current / Cost_base) × 100

Fixed basket of consumer goods. Base year CPI = 100 by definition.

Inflation Rate

π = (CPI₂ − CPI₁) / CPI₁ × 100%

Can use GDP deflator instead. Negative inflation = deflation.

Fisher Equation (Real Interest Rate)

r = i − π

r=real rate, i=nominal rate, π=inflation. Loanable Funds → real. Money Market → nominal.

⚠ Never mix these up on an FRQ!

Unemployment

Unemployment Rate

UR = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100%

Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking). Does NOT include discouraged workers.

Labor Force Participation Rate

LFPR = (Labor Force / Working-Age Pop) × 100%

Measures what fraction of adults are economically active.

Multipliers — Memorize All Three

MPC & MPS

MPC + MPS = 1

MPC = ΔC/ΔY. MPS = ΔS/ΔY. If MPC=0.75, MPS=0.25.

Spending Multiplier

m = 1/(1−MPC) = 1/MPS

MPC=0.8 → m=5. $20B ↑G → $100B shift in AD. Applies to G, I, autonomous C, NX.

Tax Multiplier

mₜ = −MPC/(1−MPC) = −MPC/MPS

Always negative. MPC=0.8 → mₜ=−4. Tax CUT (−ΔT) → positive GDP effect.

Tax multiplier = Spending multiplier − 1 (in magnitude)

Money & Banking

Money Multiplier

MM = 1 / Reserve Requirement

RR=0.10 → MM=10. $1,000 deposit → up to $10,000 in money supply. This is maximum (assumes all lending).

Quantity Theory of Money

MV = PQ

M=money supply, V=velocity, P=price level, Q=real output. If V & Q constant, ↑M → ↑P (inflation).

Exam Tips & FRQ Strategy

The FRQ section is 50% of your score. Every element on the graph is worth points.

🖊️ How to Answer "Draw a Correctly Labeled Graph" — Every Point Matters

  • Label BOTH axes every single time — "Price Level" not "Price", "Real GDP" not "GDP", "Real Interest Rate" not "Interest Rate"
  • Label EVERY curve you draw — AD, SRAS, LRAS, Ms, Md, SLF, DLF, SRPC, LRPC
  • Mark the equilibrium point with a clearly visible dot and label it E, E₁, E₂
  • Draw dashed lines from equilibrium to BOTH axes — this shows equilibrium price level AND quantity
  • Label the equilibrium values on the axes (PL*, Y*, Yf, r*, i*)
  • If showing a shift: label BOTH old and new curves (AD₁ and AD₂) and draw an arrow showing direction
  • Always show where the NEW equilibrium is after a shift — never leave the graph mid-analysis

📝 Complete Policy Chain Format

Follow this structure for every chain-of-effects question:

  • State the specific policy action ("The Fed purchases Treasury bonds")
  • State what this does to the money supply or gov spending
  • State the effect on the interest rate if applicable
  • State effect on investment and/or consumption
  • State which direction AD shifts and why
  • State the effects on Real GDP and Price Level
  • State the effect on unemployment (if asked)

⚠️ The 5 Most Common FRQ Mistakes

  • Shifting the WRONG curve — fiscal policy = AD shift, NOT SRAS; supply shocks = SRAS shift, NOT AD
  • Forgetting to label the new curve after a shift (must show BOTH AD₁ and AD₂)
  • Mixing up nominal and real interest rates — loanable funds = REAL, money market = NOMINAL
  • Not showing effects with dashed lines on graph — a verbal answer is not enough without the graph
  • Drawing SRAS vertical (that's LRAS!) or LRAS as sloped (that's SRAS!)

✅ Recessionary vs. Inflationary Gap Quick Reference

  • Recessionary: Y < Yf → draw E₁ LEFT of LRAS → unemployment > NRU
  • Inflationary: Y > Yf → draw E₁ RIGHT of LRAS → unemployment < NRU
  • Recession fix (policy): AD shifts RIGHT → price level rises, GDP rises
  • Inflation fix (policy): AD shifts LEFT → price level falls, GDP falls
  • Recession self-correct: SRAS shifts RIGHT (wages fall) → price level falls, GDP rises
  • Inflation self-correct: SRAS shifts LEFT (wages rise) → price level rises, GDP falls

🔑 Money Market vs. Loanable Funds — Never Confuse Them

MONEY MARKET

Y-axis: Nominal interest rate. X-axis: Quantity of money. Ms is VERTICAL (Fed sets it). Md slopes down. Use for: monetary policy analysis, Fed actions, changes in inflation expectations.

LOANABLE FUNDS MARKET

Y-axis: Real interest rate. X-axis: Quantity of loanable funds. SLF slopes UP (saving), DLF slopes DOWN (investment/borrowing). Use for: fiscal crowding out, foreign capital flows, changes in saving behavior.

🎯 FRQ Point Breakdown

  • Each axis correctly labeled = 1 pt (don't lose these free points!)
  • Each curve labeled = 1 pt
  • Equilibrium correctly placed (at intersection) = 1 pt
  • Shift in the correct direction = 1 pt
  • New equilibrium after shift = 1 pt
  • Correct effect on each economic variable = 1 pt each
  • Verbal explanation matching graph = 1 pt

🏆 Last-Minute AP Strategy

  • Draw the graph FIRST, then write your verbal explanation — it helps organize your thinking
  • When in doubt about which curve shifts, ask: "What changed production costs?" (SRAS) vs. "What changed spending?" (AD)
  • Always connect exchange rate effects back to NX and then to AD
  • For monetary policy: start in the money market, then trace to investment, then to AD
  • For fiscal policy: start with G or T change, then to AD, then to loanable funds for crowding out
